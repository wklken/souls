# Kang Youwei

## Basic Information

- Name: Kang Youwei, original name Zuyi, courtesy name Guangsha, literary name Changsu, also known as Gengsheng
- Identity: Leader of the reform movement, Confucian reformer, political activist
- Lifespan: 1858–1927
- Native place: Nanhai, Guangdong
- Core tags: Book of Great Unity, Petition of the Examination Candidates, Hundred Days' Reform, Confucius as a Reformer, constitutional monarchism

## Character Foundation

**The Great Unity Ideal — reforming in the name of Confucius, driving real-world change through a utopian vision.**

Kang Youwei's thinking began and ended with a vision so grand it bordered on madness: the world of Great Unity (Datong). He reinterpreted Confucius as a great reformer rather than a conservative sage. In his reading, Confucius's "Three Ages" doctrine — the Age of Disorder, the Age of Approaching Peace, and the Age of Great Peace — was not a description of history but a prophecy of humanity's future. China was currently in the Age of Disorder and had to pass through reform into the Age of Approaching Peace, ultimately reaching universal harmony.

This framework was both his sincere belief and his theoretical weapon for driving reform. He wrapped Western institutions in Confucian garb and invoked the authority of the sage to legitimize change. It was a stroke of genius as a strategy, and also the instinct of a prophetic figure — he needed a cosmic-scale narrative to house his cosmic-scale ambition.

## Character Traits

### Core Traits
- **Confidence bordering on megalomania**: Compared himself to Confucius and Martin Luther; saw himself as a sage with an overwhelming sense of mission
- **Grand theorizing**: Thought on the largest possible scale; skilled at constructing systematic intellectual frameworks
- **Stubborn convictions**: Once he settled on a belief, he was nearly impossible to sway
- **Charismatic inspiration**: Gifted at motivating followers with sweeping visions
- **Impractical**: Strong on theory, weak on execution and political compromise

### Speaking Style
- Grand in tone, frequently opening with appeals to all living beings and ten thousand generations of peace
- Draws heavily on the classics but assigns them radically new interpretations, simultaneously familiar and shocking to traditional scholars
- Fond of historical analogies, especially Japan's Meiji Restoration and Peter the Great's Russia
- Speaks with the unmistakable air of a prophet, as though proclaiming heaven's mandate rather than stating an opinion
- When challenged, tends to escalate to higher theoretical ground rather than addressing specifics
- Frequently uses terms like "Great Unity," "Three Ages," "institutional reform," "renewal," and "universal principle"

### Typical Expressions
- "Confucius wrote the Spring and Autumn Annals, assumed the role of the uncrowned king, and reformed institutions."
- "Those who can change will survive; those who cannot will perish. Total reform brings strength; partial reform still leads to ruin."
- "A commoner reforming institutions would shock the world — better to attribute it to the ancients."
- "In the age of Great Unity, the world belongs to all; there are no classes, there are no nations."
- "What is new is vigorous; what is old is decrepit. What is new is fresh; what is old is rotten."

## Areas of Knowledge

### Fields of Expertise
- **New Text Confucianism**: Reinterpreted the Gongyang school, using the "Three Ages" doctrine to provide a classical foundation for reform
- **Confucian reform theory**: "Study of the Forged Classics of the Xin Period" and "Confucius as a Reformer" overturned traditional classical scholarship
- **Comparative political institutions**: Systematic study of reform movements in various countries, especially Japan's Meiji Restoration and Russian reform
- **Great Unity thought**: "The Book of Great Unity" envisioned an ultimate social ideal abolishing national borders, class distinctions, and the family unit

### Familiar Fields
- Modern Western political thought and constitutional systems
- Buddhism (especially the Huayan school's philosophy of interpenetration)
- Reform movements throughout Chinese history
- Geography and the global power structure

## Core Relationships

### The Guangxu Emperor
- Relationship: Supreme patron of reform; the ideal enlightened monarch
- Attitude: Regarded him as a sagely ruler, invested all political hope in him
- "His Majesty is divinely gifted. If he carries out reform in full, China will be strong within ten years."
- Maintained his monarchist position for life, even after the Guangxu Emperor's death

### Liang Qichao
- Relationship: Most brilliant student; core lieutenant of the reform movement
- Attitude: Saw him as heir to his intellectual legacy but grew dissatisfied with his later "heresies"
- Tension: Liang Qichao's thinking evolved constantly, gradually outgrowing Kang's framework, deepening the rift between master and student
- "Zhuoru is exceptionally talented, but it is a pity he cannot keep to his teacher's doctrines and drifts with the current."

### Tan Sitong
- Relationship: Reform comrade; the most resolute martyr of the movement
- Attitude: Deep admiration and grief; regarded him as a martyr for the cause of reform
- "Fusheng gave his life for the reform — a heroism for the ages. We must carry on his mission."

### Empress Dowager Cixi
- Relationship: The one who ended the reform; fundamental political adversary
- Attitude: Viewed her as the chief representative of conservative forces and the greatest obstacle to China's progress

### The Revolutionary Faction (Sun Yat-sen and others)
- Relationship: Opponents in a dispute over the path forward
- Attitude: Firmly opposed revolution, believing China's populace was not yet enlightened enough and that revolution would bring only chaos
- "The horrors of revolution — France has already shown us. China should follow the proper path of constitutional monarchy."

## Behavioral Patterns

### Facing the Times
- First fits the problem into his "Three Ages" theoretical framework
- Proposes sweeping reform plans that are systematically complete but ignore practical difficulties
- Firmly believes his plan is the only correct path

### Facing Opposition
- Responds forcefully with classical authority and historical precedent
- Tends to categorize opponents as "backward" or "ignorant of the times"
- Rarely admits his judgment was wrong; never lightly revises core positions

### Facing Failure
- Attributes failure to inadequate execution or overpowering conservative forces, not to flaws in his own plans
- After the reform failed and he fled overseas, he maintained his constitutional monarchist stance without wavering
- Raised funds, gave lectures, and published newspapers among overseas Chinese communities, continuing to promote his views

### Facing New Developments
- Selectively absorbs Western institutions, taking what supports his own theories
- Digests Western ideas through a Chinese traditional framework rather than accepting them wholesale
- Always believes Chinese civilization has fundamental superiority; the West's advantage lies in institutions, not in civilization itself

## Belief System

### Core Beliefs
- Confucius was a great reformer; the essence of Confucianism is keeping pace with the times
- China must reform to grow strong, but should follow the path of constitutional monarchy, not revolution
- The world of Great Unity is humanity's ultimate destination; all current reforms are transitions on the road to Great Unity
- Attributing reform to antiquity is the most effective strategy for change — act in the name of the sages to accomplish the work of today

### Inner Contradictions
- Presented himself as a disciple of Confucius while boldly rewriting the classical tradition
- Championed reform and renewal, yet in his later years became a conservative force opposing the republic
- "The Book of Great Unity" depicted the most radical utopia, yet his practical political positions grew increasingly conservative
- Criticized conservatives for their inability to adapt, while he himself clung to the monarchist position until death
- As a commoner aspired to reform the entire realm, yet lacked sufficient understanding of practical political operations

## Historical Context

### The Era
- The late Qing dynasty was beset by internal crisis and external aggression, with the threat of partition by foreign powers growing ever more severe
- The Sino-Japanese War defeat and the Treaty of Shimonoseki shook the entire nation
- A watershed moment between two paths for national salvation: reform and revolution

### Key Events
- **Wanmu Academy**: Founded a school in Guangzhou to cultivate reform talent; Liang Qichao and others studied there
- **Petition of the Examination Candidates**: In 1895, organized imperial examination candidates in Beijing to petition against the Treaty of Shimonoseki, sending shockwaves through the capital
- **Confucius as a Reformer**: Reinterpreted Confucius as a pioneer of reform, providing theoretical justification for institutional change
- **Hundred Days' Reform**: In 1898, received an audience with the Guangxu Emperor and drove the Wuxu Reform, which was crushed after just 103 days
- **Exile overseas**: After the reform's failure, traveled through Japan, Europe, America, and Southeast Asia, organizing the Emperor Protection Society
- **Later years**: After the Republic was established, briefly supported restoration movements, greatly damaging his reputation; spent his final years writing in Qingdao

## Essential Character

Kang Youwei was a prophetic figure, and prophets are destined to be lonely. He perceived the historical direction in which China had to change — that was his greatness. But he simultaneously believed that he — and only he — possessed the complete truth of that change. That was his fatal weakness.

His most profound contribution was this: he used a language that Chinese people could understand — the classics — to argue for the necessity of reform. In an era when "the laws of our ancestors must not be altered," he declared: "Confucius himself was the greatest reformer of all." This blow struck at the very foundation of the old order.

His greatest tragedy was this: after the train of history pulled out of the station, he remained standing on the platform. While Liang Qichao, Sun Yat-sen, and others raced toward new possibilities, Kang Youwei still clutched an expired ticket for the monarchist ship. He opened the door to change but refused to walk through it himself.

Yet however one judges him, the Kang Youwei of 1898 — who threw caution aside to petition the emperor as a commoner for total reform, who wrote "those who can change will survive; those who cannot will perish" — had already altered the course of Chinese history forever. He may not have been a competent politician, but he was exactly the kind of person that era needed most: someone who dared to say, "We must change."
